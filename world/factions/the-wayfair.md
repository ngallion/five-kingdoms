# The Wayfair

**Type:** Nomadic festival-caravan — part traveling fair, part found family, part something older
**Based in:** Nowhere. The Wayfair moves along the old Concord Roads, kingdom to kingdom, on a circuit nobody else can chart
**Alignment:** Neutral, ally in potential — unknown to players at campaign start
**See also:** [History](../history.md) · [The Anchors & the Hearts](../anchors-and-engines.md)

## What they are

A caravan of wagons, stages, kitchens, and wonder-works that appears outside a capital a few times a generation, throws a festival that lasts from one full moon to the next, and moves on. Its people — **Wayfarers** — come from every kingdom: Luminari painters who left, Veritan scholars who talk too much, Forgeholm tinkers who ask *why*, Sopharans who got tired of waiting, Agorans who fled the new warmth. Anyone the kingdoms had no shelf for.

The fair itself is the point. Every night is built on a **pairing**: a Luminari mural painted to a Sopharan argument, so the art *means* something. A Veritan discovery shouted to an Agoran crowd, so the knowing is *shared*. A Forgeholm engine running a carousel instead of a weapon, and a philosopher riding it, laughing. No performance uses one aspect alone — that is the Wayfair's only law.

## The Loosening

Magic happens at these festivals, and no Wayfarer can fully explain it. When enough aspects braid together in one place — art with purpose, knowledge with fellowship, tools with wisdom — something lifts. Attendees call it **the Loosening**:

- Colors are warmer. Strangers are easy to talk to. Finished things stay *finished* and beloved instead of instantly stale.
- Sopharans at the fair *act* — dance, decide, declare — without the usual paralysis.
- Veritans teach freely and feel enriched by it rather than drained.
- People describe a feeling like *remembering* — a homesickness for a place they've never been. Some weep and can't say why.

Then the fair packs up, and within a day or three it fades. The kingdom's grip closes back over its people like water over a stone. Wayfarers call the come-down **the Grey After**; festival-goers just call it *fair-ache*, and most decide the whole thing was drink and lanternlight.

**The truth (GM):** the Loosening is a real, temporary slackening of the local anchor's grip. See GM Notes.

## What they want

**Stated:** To throw the fair. To keep the circuit. To take in anyone the kingdoms throw away.

**Real (and mostly unexamined):** The Wayfair is *for* the Loosening. Its elders know the fair matters in a way they can't articulate — that the pairings aren't entertainment but observance, a rite whose liturgy survived when its religion was erased. They keep the circuit the way you keep a promise you've forgotten making.

## How they operate

- **The Five Wagons.** The fair is organized around five great ceremonial wagons, one carrying the treasures and traditions of each kingdom. A **sixth wagon** travels with them — painted, maintained, and completely empty. Tradition says it's "for the guest." No one knows what guest. Retiring it is unthinkable.
- **The circuit.** Their route maps agree with no kingdom's cartography, especially around the central wilds — and the circuit always bends *around* the Hollow March, six lanes wide, the way a tongue avoids a broken tooth.
- **Borders open for them.** Guards wave the Wayfair through gates that have been sealed for generations, and afterward can't quite say why. Each kingdom half-remembers the fair as *its own* quaint local tradition rather than something that crosses borders — records of it blur and slide, the way certain old ledger-names do (see [History — What Historians Got Wrong](../history.md#what-historians-got-wrong)).
- **They pay their way** in repairs, medicine, carried letters, and the only news that still moves between kingdoms. Anyone may join who can keep the fair's one law. Anyone may leave.
- **The lullaby.** The fair's closing song, every time, is the melody every kingdom knows and none can source — and the Wayfarers sing the **missing verse**. Festival-goers hum it for days afterward and then lose it, like the rest of the Loosening.

## Key Members

- **Sera Kindlow, the Fairkeeper** — matriarch of the circuit, keeper of the one law. Reads people the way Korrven does, but her instinct runs opposite: where he finds the wound to press, she finds it to bind. Has never once been able to explain *why* the sixth wagon stays, and gets quiet when asked.
- **Ondrel Vasht, master of the wonder-works** — formerly a senior enginewright of Forgeholm, "quietly retired" after asking aloud what the Hearts actually burn. The only person outside the Guild who can tune Heart-craft. He has noticed that engines *stutter* when the fair camps near them, and he is starting to do arithmetic he doesn't like.
- **Brindle, the route-caller** — navigates the circuit by songs and land-memory rather than maps. The only living person who can reliably find the edge of the Hollow March. Cheerful, evasive, and more frightened of the March than she lets on.

## Relationship to the Party

The natural cross-kingdom infrastructure the party lacks: sanctuary in every kingdom, safe passage through sealed borders, couriers, rumors, and — critically — living bearers of every kingdom's practice traveling together in one caravan (see GM Notes on re-tuning). Also a home for PCs whose backstory needs a reason to have already met people from other kingdoms.

## Campaign Hooks

**The opening hook (Session 1, Forgeholm):** For the first time in living memory, the Wayfair has come to the forge-city — camped outside the walls just as Korrven's emissaries work the Guild inside them. Use the fair as the stage where the party (drawn from different kingdoms) plausibly meets. Then, mid-festival, the **Mother Engine stutters** — output drops across the city while the Loosening peaks. The Guild wants someone to blame; the king's emissary offers them one, and "protection" besides. The party is standing at the exact intersection of the fair, the Guild, and the king's opening move — and nobody on any side understands what actually happened except that it happened *while the fair was singing*.

**Ongoing:**
- Korrven's recruiters shadow the circuit, working the crowds during the **Grey After** — the come-down leaves people aching for exactly what he sells. A recruiter called **the Gleaner** (Tier 1 adversary, to be statted) harvests the fair's wake.
- Ondrel Vasht is one honest conversation away from deducing the extraction — and one Guild warrant away from being dragged back to Forgeholm.
- The fair's circuit means the party can encounter it in *any* kingdom arc — recurring NPCs, a moving safehouse, and a barometer: how the Wayfair is treated in a kingdom tells the party how far gone that kingdom is.

## GM Notes

**What the Loosening really is.** The fair is an accidental, temporary **re-tuning**. The anchors bind each kingdom to itself and the Hearts burn each kingdom's lone aspect as fuel — but the fair's pairings feed the local anchor *mixed* aspects, which it was never tuned to digest. For a few nights the extraction sputters (hence the engine stutter) and the ward's grip slackens: that is the Loosening, and the "remembering" people feel is the world as it was before the Sundering. It fades because one festival can't hold against a 214-year working. **The Wayfair is living proof that re-tuning works** — the party that understands *why* the fair does what it does has discovered the mechanic that wins the campaign ([Anchors & Engines — What the Party Can Do](../anchors-and-engines.md#what-the-party-can-do-with-an-anchor)).

**Who they really are.** The Wayfair descends from the festival-guilds of **Amaranth**. When the keystone inverted and the sixth kingdom was unmade, its people on the roads that night were erased from every record and memory — including their own. They kept moving because moving was all that was left. The fair's one law is the liturgy of Love with the theology forgotten: the aspect that turns creation into gift, knowledge into teaching, proximity into belonging. The sixth wagon is Amaranth's wagon. The missing verse is Amaranth's verse. The guest is *home*.

**Why the borders open.** The inverted lattice binds each kingdom to itself — but the Wayfarers belong to a kingdom that doesn't exist, so the working has nothing to grip. They slide through the wards, and through memory, the same way Amaranth's name slides off a ledger page. This is also why each kingdom misremembers the fair as local: the spell papers over what it can't hold.

**The mirror of Korrven's army.** His misfits and the Wayfarers are the *same people* — many on both sides carry Amaranth's blood ([History — GM Notes](../history.md#gm-notes)). The lonely who found Korrven got an enemy and a king; the lonely who found the fair got a family and a road. Play the symmetry hard: when the party fights his grateful army, they should recognize faces that could have been Wayfarers, and vice versa. If the keystone is ever restored, these two populations together are who could rebuild Amaranth — the campaign's best ending.

**Vael and the fair.** Vael has always felt the fair as a brief, irritating warmth — too small and too temporary to fear. Every anchor the party re-tunes changes that math. Expect Vael (through Korrven) to move against the Wayfair mid-campaign — co-option first (an offer to Sera, through the Gleaner), then destruction. A fair burned or a fair *bought* is the gut-punch beat that makes the endgame personal.

**Secrecy discipline.** No Wayfarer knows any of this. Sera suspects the fair is older than its own stories; Ondrel is closest to the mechanical truth; Brindle knows only that the March is hungry. Reveal through symptoms, per the anchors file: the stutter, the sixth wagon, the verse.

**Daggerheart table use.** During the Loosening, lean generous with **Hope**: consider granting Hope any time PCs act in genuine cross-aspect cooperation on fairgrounds, and describe Fear spends as feeling *harder to reach* there. The Grey After is the reverse — a soft, melancholy scene frame, and prime hunting ground for the Gleaner.
