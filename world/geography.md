## The Shape of the World

The oldest charts — the ones rotting unread in Veritas's vaults — call the continent **the Concord**, and draw it as a wheel: kingdoms arranged around a rich central land, joined by a ring of roads at the rim and great spoke-roads running inward to the hub. Modern maps draw no wheel. Each kingdom's official cartography places *itself* at the center of the world and lets the edges go vague — and where the hub should be, every map shows something different, or nothing at all.

The land itself still remembers the old shape. Five kingdoms ring a center no one can chart. The continent faces inward, toward a place nobody goes.

Beyond the rim, the world simply ends as far as anyone knows: ice north of Forgeholm's peaks, trackless badlands east of the Veritan plateau, open ocean — the **Vesper Sea** — west past Luminar's cliffs. Only Luminar has a charted coast, and even Luminar's ships hug it. Nobody sails out. Nobody ever has, that the records show — one more thing the records are strangely incurious about.

## Regions

### Forgeholm — the Ironspine *(north)*

**Location:** The mountain wall across the top of the world; north of it, only ice.
**Climate/terrain:** High peaks and deep-cut valleys, rich in ore and coal. Long brutal winters. The air over the cities hazes with foundry smoke; snow greys before it lands.
**Notable features:** The forge-city of **Kaldrum**, capital and Guild seat, built *into* a mountain as much as on it — terraces of workshops descending to the Mother Engine at its root. Rail-sleds and cable-lifts move ore where no road could. The slag-tips of two centuries form artificial hills the locals call the Made Range.
**Who lives here:** Enginewrights, miners, operators — and the operated. Nothing grows at altitude; Forgeholm feeds itself with machine-worked valley farms and vast fungus-cellars, and it is prouder of that than it should be.

Forgeholm's geography is its argument: the mountains gave it everything it needed and a wall against everyone else. See [Forgeholm](kingdoms/forgeholm.md).

### Veritas — the Tablelands *(east)*

**Location:** A vast high plateau east of the Ironspine's tail, in the mountains' rain shadow.
**Climate/terrain:** Arid, cold-nighted, and impossibly clear — the best skies in the world, which is why the observatories are here. Salt flats along the plateau's heart mirror the stars so perfectly that early astronomers charted half the sky looking *down*. Deep beneath the rock: crystal caverns and the coldest, driest vaults in the world — ideal for keeping paper a very long time.
**Notable features:** The capital, **Meridian**, a city of domes and instrument-towers laid out on a precise survey grid, its streets aligned to solstice sunrise. The Great Archive descends more levels below ground than the towers rise above it.
**Who lives here:** Scholars, archivists, instrument-makers, and the quiet farming towns of the plateau's irrigated fringe who feed them and are barely acknowledged.

The plateau's isolation is real but partial — the truth is the Silent Heart, not the altitude. See [Veritas](kingdoms/veritas.md).

### Sophara — the Meander Country *(south)*

**Location:** The mild south — low hills and river country between the plateau's descent and the coastal fens.
**Climate/terrain:** The gentlest climate in the world. The great slow river — **the Meander** — braids apart and rejoins across a wide green valley of terraced groves and vineyards. Warm summers, soft winters, reliable rain. A land that has never once forced anyone to decide anything quickly.
**Notable features:** The capital, called **the Stoa**, a city of a thousand linked courtyards, colonnades, and debate-gardens spread along both banks of the river's main division. Which branch of the Meander is the *true* river has been formally disputed for four hundred years; there is a school for each position.
**Who lives here:** Philosophers, rhetors, students, and the patient farmers of the valley — the only people in Sophara who finish things.

The comfort of the land is part of the trap: nothing here has ever pushed back. See [Sophara](kingdoms/sophara.md).

### Luminar — the Glassreach *(west)*

**Location:** The western coast — the only charted shore in the world — where the land shatters into sea-cliffs, lagoons, and a scatter of islands.
**Climate/terrain:** Mild and maritime, famous above all for its *light*: the long gold of western evenings, storm-skies that painters ruin themselves chasing, winter fogs that turn the lagoon cities into rumors. The cliffs are streaked with colored mineral bands, and the beaches below them supply the glassworks.
**Notable features:** The capital, **Prismere**, built across a drowned river-mouth — bridges, stages, and glass towers rising from tidal water, every surface painted, mosaicked, or mirrored, and repainted before it can become familiar. Whole islands of the archipelago are single abandoned artworks.
**Who lives here:** Artists, performers, glasswrights, luthiers, inventors — and the fisherfolk and bargemen who keep them fed and are the only steady thing in the kingdom.

Luminar's beauty is compulsive, not serene; the light is the one masterpiece nobody can finish or discard. See [Luminar](kingdoms/luminar.md).

### Agoran — the Granary *(northwest)*

**Location:** The wide plains between Luminar's chalk hills and Forgeholm's foothills — the shortest border with Forgeholm of any kingdom, which matters a great deal just now.
**Climate/terrain:** Open grassland under a huge sky. Continental weather with real teeth: hot summers, hard winters, and the harvest-race between them. This is the land that *taught* cooperation — no household survives a Granary winter alone, and the ring-barns and shared granaries are older than the kingdom itself.
**Notable features:** The capital, **Commonhold**, less a city than a hundred villages grown together around the great moot-ring where all of Agoran once gathered at harvest. The long-table feasts survive. What's spoken over them has changed.
**Who lives here:** Farmers, herders, drovers, wardens — a people built for solidarity, which is exactly what made them the first to fall. The king's banners fly from the ring-barns now, and the border wardens face *outward*.

Five years under Korrven have made Agoran orderly, grateful, and armed. See [Agoran](kingdoms/agoran.md) and [the Fall of Agoran](history.md#the-fall-of-agoran-209-as).

## Major Trade Routes

### The Concord Roads

The pre-Sundering road network, built in the Age of the Weave and never equaled since. Two parts:

- **The spoke-roads** — five great highways, one from each capital, running inward toward the center of the wheel. Superbly engineered, uncannily preserved (pre-Sundering craft; frost doesn't heave them and weeds don't take them), and completely dead: every one of them stops at the edge of the Hollow March, **six lanes wide, going nowhere**. Nobody can say why a road would be built six lanes wide when there have only ever been five kingdoms. At each dead end stands a ruined waystation-plaza — locals near the March edge call them **the Stubs** — big enough to stage a festival in, which once, they did.
- **The Girdle** — the rim road linking the five capitals in a great ring, kingdom to kingdom around the outside of the March. Decayed but walkable: two centuries of sealed borders have left it cracked, flooded in the fens, drifted over in the Screes. The Wayfarers, who use it more than anyone alive, just call it **the Round**.

### Who still moves

Almost no one. Every border-gate on the Girdle has been sealed for generations — actual walls and customs-forts at the five crossings, manned by guards enforcing a closure none of them can explain the original reason for. Traffic that still flows:

- **[The Wayfair](factions/the-wayfair.md)** — waved through every sealed gate by guards who afterward can't say why. The only news, letters, and small trade that reliably crosses borders.
- **Smugglers and guides** — thin, expensive, dangerous. Every border wild has its crossing-paths and the specialists who know them.
- **Exiles and the aspectless** — outbound only, as far as the kingdoms care. The wilds between the kingdoms are where the sorted world's leftovers end up.
- **The king's columns** — the newest traffic on the old roads. Agoran's wardens now patrol the Girdle east toward Forgeholm, and repair crews have been seen *fixing* it. Nobody fixes a road they don't intend to march down.

## Travel Times

Rules of thumb at Girdle pace, assuming you can cross the borders at all (times are for the Wayfair, smugglers, and player characters — official travel effectively doesn't exist):

| Leg (neighboring capitals, via the Girdle) | By wagon | Hard riding |
|---|---|---|
| Commonhold (Agoran) ↔ Kaldrum (Forgeholm) | ~2 weeks | 6–7 days |
| Kaldrum (Forgeholm) ↔ Meridian (Veritas) | ~3 weeks | 9–10 days |
| Meridian (Veritas) ↔ the Stoa (Sophara) | ~4 weeks | ~12 days |
| the Stoa (Sophara) ↔ Prismere (Luminar) | ~3 weeks | ~10 days |
| Prismere (Luminar) ↔ Commonhold (Agoran) | ~3 weeks | 9–10 days |

- **Crossing the wheel** (e.g., Forgeholm to Sophara) means going *around*: two legs, 6–8 weeks by wagon. The dead spoke-roads would cut any crossing to under three weeks — straight through the Hollow March. Nobody does. When players ask why nobody does, that's the point.
- **Agoran ↔ Forgeholm is the shortest leg on the ring.** Korrven did not pick his first two kingdoms at random.
- **The Wayfair's circuit** runs the full Round in about two and a half years — a fair outside a different capital every solstice, meandering pace, long stops. Their route-songs are more accurate than any kingdom's maps, especially near the March.
- Weather levers: Ironspine passes close from first snow to thaw (add weeks or go around); the Meander floods in spring; Screes rust-storms stop everything for days.

## Dangerous Wilds

The border wilds are the unclaimed lands between kingdoms — too far from every Heart to be anyone's problem, which makes them the only places in the world nobody is watching. Exiles, the aspectless, smugglers, and stranger things live in them.

And the stranger things are multiplying. For a generation the wilds have worsened; for the last five years the worsening has had teeth — roads closing, waystations dark, the March's horrors turning up where they never ranged before. See [The Hollowing](the-hollowing.md).

### The Hollow March *(center)*

The wild region at the center of the world, where all the old Concord Roads converge and stop dead. No two maps agree on its shape or size; cartographers gave up generations ago. Travelers who skirt its edge report a wrongness they can't name — a city-shaped absence on the horizon that isn't there when looked at directly.

**GM truth:** this is where **Amaranth**, the unmade sixth kingdom, stood. Its keystone anchor still hums at the center. See [History](history.md) and [The Anchors & the Hearts](anchors-and-engines.md). The March is also the source of the rising tide — it has been breathing its things out into the border wilds since 209 ([The Hollowing](the-hollowing.md)).

### The Thornfell *(Agoran ↔ Forgeholm)*

High moorland where the plains buckle into Forgeholm's foothills, choked with iron-briar — thorn hedges that blunt axes and grow back overnight. For two centuries the refuge of exiles and the unsorted; **this is where Korrven's army of outcasts gathered before the Fall of Agoran**, and its watchfires are burning again. The shortest leg of the Girdle runs straight through it.

### The Screes *(Forgeholm ↔ Veritas)*

The Ironspine's collapsing eastern tail: wind-scoured scree fields, ravines fouled orange with two hundred years of slag runoff, and the **rust-storms** — wind-borne grit that flays paint, skin, and lungs. The emptiest of the border wilds; even exiles mostly pass through.

### The Hush *(Veritas ↔ Sophara)*

The long grass steppe down the plateau's southern descent, where sound behaves wrongly: a shout dies at ten paces on one hill and a whisper carries a mile from the next. Veritan acoustic surveys of the Hush never produce the same measurements twice, a fact Veritas finds so upsetting it has stopped surveying. Herders who graze its edges never, ever sing there.

### The Sighing Fens *(Sophara ↔ Luminar)*

The tidal marsh where the Meander's many mouths tangle into Luminar's lagoon country. Fog, drowned causeways, marsh-lights that Luminari painters chase and sometimes follow too far — and things in the fog that imitate voices. The Girdle crosses on a raised pre-Sundering causeway that floods anyway.

### The Chalklands *(Luminar ↔ Agoran)*

Bone-white rolling downs that neither kingdom has ever claimed, cut with enormous ancient hill-figures no kingdom's history mentions making. The largest, visible only from the high downs opposite, is a **ring of six dancing figures holding hands**. Every kingdom's records, when they mention the figures at all, describe a ring of five. Shepherds' folklore says it's bad luck to count them.

## GM Notes

**The wheel is theology made landscape.** Love sat at the center and everything faced it; the spoke-roads were built for festival-pilgrimage inward as much as trade. Post-Sundering, every kingdom redrew the map with itself at the center — the cartography *is* the isolation. Only three sources preserve the true wheel: the pre-Sundering charts in Veritas's vaults, the Wayfair's route-songs, and the land itself.

**Space is knotted near the March.** The continent is sized for six kingdoms and holds five — surveyors' triangulations near the center never close, distances refuse to sum, and the March's measured size disagrees with itself. This is the unmaking's scar tissue, and it's why Brindle of the Wayfair navigates by song and land-memory instead of maps ([the Wayfair](factions/the-wayfair.md)). Use it at the table: any party that crosses the March's edge should find that time, distance, and direction stop being trustworthy.

**Player-discoverable cracks seeded in this file** (companions to [What Historians Got Wrong](history.md#what-historians-got-wrong)): the six-lane spoke-roads; the Stubs (plazas big enough for a festival, at the end of roads to nowhere); the sixth dancer in the Chalklands ring; the Girdle's five border-forts enforcing a closure nobody can source; a continent that faces inward toward nothing.

**The map as war news.** Geography should make Korrven's strategy legible on sight: Agoran shares the shortest border with Forgeholm, his wardens patrol the Girdle eastward, and his crews are repairing the road. Players with the handout map can work out the next target themselves — let them.

**Names are levers.** Capital names (**Kaldrum, Meridian, the Stoa, Prismere, Commonhold**) and wild names are coined here for the first time — rename freely before they appear in play; this file and the handout map are currently their only references. Travel times are pacing levers, not physics: stretch or shrink them per arc.

**The Vesper Sea question.** Nobody sails out and no record wonders why — deliberately open. Options: the ward-lattice turns ships gently home (the inverted Binding binds each kingdom inward); there's genuinely nothing out there; or something pre-Sundering is out there and Vael prefers it forgotten. Decide only if a player tries it.
