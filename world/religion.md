# Religion — The Six

**Nature:** The old faith of the Concord, and the five warped cults it has become
**Public knowledge:** Every kingdom knows its own god. Almost no one remembers they were ever worshipped together.
**See also:** [History](history.md) · [The Anchors & the Hearts](anchors-and-engines.md) · [Vael, the Unmade](vael-the-unmade.md) · [The Wayfair](factions/the-wayfair.md)

## The Old Faith — the Concord of Six

Before the Sundering, the kingdoms shared one religion with six gods — the **Concord of Six**, called simply **the Weave** in daily speech. Each god was the pure embodiment of one aspect of humanity, and each kingdom rose around its god's great gathering-place (see [History — the Founding](history.md#timeline)). But the Weave had one commandment, and everything else was commentary:

> **No god is worshipped alone.**

Every rite paired at least two gods. A work of art was consecrated to Ora *and* Sophar — the spark and the purpose. A proof was sealed to Theora *and* Tavan — the truth and the telling of it. To pray to one god only was not heresy; it was considered *impossible*, like clapping with one hand. The gods themselves were said to keep this law: the creation myth has no solo acts.

**The creation, as the Weave told it:** In the beginning was the dark, which was old, and patient, and does not come into this story yet. Ora struck the first spark. Theora watched how it burned, and learned the laws of its burning. Ferrun caught it and forged the world on it. Sophar weighed the world and asked what it was for. Tavan built the long table and called everyone in from the dark to live in it. *And Amara — Amara is why they did it together.*

The sixth name in that myth no longer appears in any version told in any kingdom. The verse that carried it is the missing verse of the lullaby every kingdom knows and cannot source ([History — What Historians Got Wrong](history.md#what-historians-got-wrong)).

**The solstice festivals** were the Weave's high holy days — every capital at once, gods invoked in pairs, the roads full of pilgrims moving *inward* along the spokes. The only observance of the old calendar left in the world is the [Wayfair](factions/the-wayfair.md)'s fair, which still raises itself every solstice without knowing why.

## The Six at a Glance

| God | Aspect | Kingdom | Old title | What the cult has become |
|-----|--------|---------|-----------|--------------------------|
| **Ora** | Creativity | Luminar | the First Spark | worship of beginnings; finishing is profane |
| **Theora** | Science | Veritas | the Clear Eye | the Silent Witness — shown everything, telling no one |
| **Ferrun** | Technology | Forgeholm | the Shaping Hand | the Exacting — the god of *works*, stripped of *why* |
| **Sophar** | Philosophy | Sophara | the Weigher | the Undecided — verdicts became heresy |
| **Tavan** | Community | Agoran | the Long Table | the King's Table — Korrven sits in the god's chair |
| *(a sixth?)* | — | — | — | *a blank space in every kingdom's rites; see **the Guest customs*** |

Since the Sundering, each kingdom remembers a different pantheon — always five gods, always with **its own god at the head** and the other four demoted to servants, aspects, or foreign superstitions. No two kingdoms' theologies agree, and none of them notices the seam where a sixth name was.

## Ora, the First Spark *(Luminar — Creativity)*

**Domains:** inspiration, invention, art, the blank page, the held breath before the first stroke
**Symbol:** a single spark on a dark field; in older iconography, a spark *passing between two hands*
**Depicted as:** a figure whose face is never finished — every Luminari artist leaves her incomplete, on purpose, so the next artist has something to do

Ora is creativity entire: the god who has never once repeated herself. Every dawn is her rough draft; she is said to have invented color twice because she didn't like it the first time. Her old teaching was that the spark is a *gift in transit* — inspiration passes through you on its way to someone else, and hoarding it snuffs it.

**Worship then:** works were begun in Ora's name and *finished* in another god's — dedicated to Sophar's purpose, or laid on Tavan's table. The finishing was the worship.
**Worship now:** Luminar prays only at beginnings. Ground-breakings, first strokes, maiden voyages — these are sacred. Finishing a work has become quietly obscene, something done in private and not spoken of; there is no rite for completion at all. Luminari say *"Ora keep it new."* They believe this is piety. It is withdrawal ([the Heart of a Thousand Doors](anchors-and-engines.md#luminar--the-heart-of-a-thousand-doors) drinks completion).
**Her temple** in Prismere is rebuilt every year, on principle, by a different architect. It has never once been completed before demolition begins — this is now the point, though no one can say when it became the point.

## Theora, the Clear Eye *(Veritas — Science)*

**Domains:** observation, measurement, stars, the moment of understanding, honest instruments
**Symbol:** an open eye with a lens for a pupil; older versions show the eye *reflected in a second eye*
**Depicted as:** a still figure looking slightly past you, at something you can't see yet

Theora is the aspect of knowing at its purest: the god who sees what *is*, without flinching and without preference. She is said to have watched Ora's first spark burn for a thousand years before saying anything, and what she finally said was exact. Her old teaching: a truth is not finished until it is *given* — the eye takes in light and must give it back, or it is only a stone.

**Worship then:** discovery as devotion, and *teaching* as the sacrament. A scholar's masterwork was the lecture, not the text — truth passed voice to voice, sealed to Theora and Tavan together.
**Worship now:** Veritas worships **the Silent Witness**. Scholars complete a work and *show it to Theora alone* — a private rite, manuscript held open toward the temple, then vaulted forever. Confession with no absolution; publication with no reader. Veritans have come to believe silence is what she wants, because speaking their work aloud genuinely diminishes it ([the Silent Heart](anchors-and-engines.md#veritas--the-silent-heart) drinks the transfer — the piety is a symptom).
**Her temple** in Meridian is the observatory at the survey-grid's zero point, aligned to solstice sunrise. Its lower vaults are contiguous with the Great Archive — the church and the unread library are the same building, which no one finds strange.

## Ferrun, the Shaping Hand *(Forgeholm — Technology)*

**Domains:** craft, engineering, tools, load-bearing things, the promise a made thing makes
**Symbol:** a hand holding a hammer; older versions show the hand *open*, offering the hammer grip-first
**Depicted as:** a broad figure with burn-scarred forearms and a maker's mark on every knuckle

Ferrun is technology itself: the god whose worship is *work that holds*. He does not accept prayers in words — he accepts joints that don't fail, bridges that bear, blades that keep their edge. His old teaching was carved over every guild door in the world: **a tool is a promise to the hand that will use it.** The promise, not the tool, was the sacred thing.

**Worship then:** the finished work presented at the forge-altar with its *purpose spoken aloud* — this plow for the Granary, this lens for Meridian, this bell for the fair. Making, consecrated to the made-for.
**Worship now:** Forgeholm worships **Ferrun the Exacting** — the god of tolerance and gauge, who asks only *does it work*, never *what is it for*. His litanies survive as safety procedure, recited exactly and meaning nothing. Guild oaths are still sworn on his anvil. The over-door carving is still everywhere, read by no one, like a maker's mark on the world ([the Mother Engine](anchors-and-engines.md#forgeholm--the-first-heart-the-mother-engine) drinks the *why* out of labor).
**His temple** is Kaldrum's Guildhall — or rather, the Guildhall stands in what was the temple, and the Mother Engine occupies the old sanctum at the mountain's root. The enginewrights who found "something that hummed" down there sixty years ago were digging in Ferrun's holiest ground, and not one record finds this worth mentioning.

## Sophar, the Weigher *(Sophara — Philosophy)*

**Domains:** wisdom, ethics, judgment, the well-formed question, the moment of verdict
**Symbol:** a balance-scale; older versions show the scale *settled*, one pan touching down
**Depicted as:** a seated figure with one hand open (the question) and one closed (the answer) — modern Sopharan statues have both hands open

Sophar is philosophy whole: the god who asks the question under the question, and — this is the part Sophara has forgotten — the god who *answers*. The old faith held that Sophar's scale exists to come to rest: a weighing that never ends isn't wisdom, it's cowardice with a library. Sophara is the only kingdom that bears its god's own name, a fact its scholars consider etymologically disputed. It isn't.

**Worship then:** the year's great disputes argued all season and *settled* at solstice — the Verdict, Sophara's high rite, after which the settled thing was acted on, with Ferrun's tools and Tavan's hands, before the next year's questions opened.
**Worship now:** Sophara worships **Sophar the Undecided**. To close a question is to kill it, the schools now teach; verdicts are for lesser minds. The last recorded Verdict was 213 years ago. The scale on every altar is carefully balanced to touch nothing, and Sopharans feel a bitterness in front of it they do not examine ([the Still Heart](anchors-and-engines.md#sophara--the-still-heart) drinks the moment between decision and act — the theology is anesthesia for the theft).
**His temple** is the oldest debate-garden in the Stoa, at the disputed fork of the Meander. Both branches claim it. The dispute is four hundred years old and is, of course, undecided.

## Tavan, the Long Table *(Agoran — Community)*

**Domains:** hospitality, harvest, thresholds, shared labor, the stranger made kin
**Symbol:** an open door; older versions show a table seen through it, with **one empty chair**
**Depicted as:** a big weathered figure carrying a bench under one arm — always *arriving*, never enthroned

Tavan is community incarnate: the god of the door that opens and the table that lengthens. His arithmetic was the Granary's oldest law — *a table of ten feeds eleven* — and his one absolute commandment concerned the stranger at the threshold: feed them first, ask their name after. He is the only god of the Six traditionally shown standing, because a god of belonging has no throne; he sits wherever there's room.

**Worship then:** the harvest moot at Commonhold's great ring, the long-table feasts, the empty chair kept at every table — set with a full plate — *for the guest*. Ask an old Agoran who the guest is and you'd have gotten a shrug and a refill: the guest is whoever comes.
**Worship now:** Agoran worships at **the King's Table**. Korrven has not banned Tavan — he has *moved in with him*. Grace over the long tables now names the king in the god's cadences; the moot-ring where all Agoran gathered is where Korrven holds audience; the border wardens say *"the door holds"* as a blessing, and mean the door stays **shut**. Hospitality inverted into vigilance, belonging into enlistment ([the Warm Heart](anchors-and-engines.md#agoran--the-warm-heart-now-the-kings-heart) drank the warmth; the king supplies the substitute). The empty chair survives at Agoran tables even now — though under Korrven it is coming to mean *the king's place, kept ready*, which is not what it meant.
**His temple** was never a building. It was the moot-ring, the ring-barns, and the practice itself — which is why it was so easy to occupy.

## The Guest Customs

Five small habits, one per kingdom, that every kingdom keeps and none can explain. They appear in no scripture. Ask why, and people say *it's just how it's done*:

- **Luminar:** finished works are signed with the artist's mark *and a blank space beside it* — "the collaborator's corner." Filling it is terrible luck.
- **Veritas:** every completed manuscript opens with an empty dedication page. Catalogued as "dedication: unassigned," all of them, for two hundred years.
- **Forgeholm:** every certified work bears two maker's stamps — the wright's own, and beside it a blank die that marks nothing. Guild regulation requires the blank stamp. No regulation says why.
- **Sophara:** every formal debate reserves the final word for "the absent speaker" — a held silence before any (theoretical) verdict. The schools agree on nothing except that the silence stays.
- **Agoran:** the empty chair, the full plate, the guest who is whoever comes.

Set beside the [sixth wagon](factions/the-wayfair.md#how-they-operate), the six-lane roads, and the sixth dancer in the Chalklands ([Geography](geography.md#the-chalklands-luminar--agoran)), the shape of the blank space is not hard to see — for players. See GM Notes.

## The Silence of the Gods

Here is the fact every faith lives with and none advertises: **no god has answered since the Sundering.** No miracle, no dream-omen, no voice in the sanctum in 214 years. Each kingdom's theology explains the silence in its own image — Ora is *mid-work* and must not be interrupted; Theora watches and it would corrupt the observation to speak; Ferrun's answer *is* the standing world, what more do you want; Sophar is still weighing; Tavan (Agorans now say) finally answered, and his answer wore a crown.

Divine magic still works — clerics and seraphs channel their god's *aspect*, and the aspect is real even where the god is silent. Most clergy have quietly stopped expecting anything on the other end of prayer. The ones who haven't stopped are the interesting ones.

## Vael in Folk Memory — the Hollow One

The old faith had no devil. It had the dark the first spark was struck in — old, patient, previous — and it taught, without much alarm, that the dark is still there and still patient, and that the Weave itself was the wall against it: *what is held is not taken.*

The kingdoms' fairy tales remember more than their theologies do. Every kingdom scares its children with the same figure under different names — **the Hollow One** (Agoran), **the Unpainted** (Luminar), **the Unread** (Veritas), **Old Empty** (Forgeholm), **the Unasked** (Sophara). The stories always agree on three things: it cannot open a door itself; it is drawn to the child who sulks *alone*; and it does not eat you — it eats your *place*, so that no one comes looking, because no one remembers there is anyone to look for. Parents tell these tales as nonsense. Every detail in them is true ([Vael, the Unmade](vael-the-unmade.md)).

## GM Notes

**The gods are real** — real the way Vael is real: elder concentrations of aspect, older than the kingdoms, each the living embodiment of one strand of what humans are. Vael is *not* one of them and never was; it is the absence they were the wall against. The kingdoms of the Founding coalesced around the gods' gathering-places — the "great gathering-places of their aspects" in [History](history.md#timeline) were the gods' seats.

**The gods are in the anchors.** The Anchor Working was consecrated, not just engineered: at activation, each god stood at its own anchor as guarantor — six pillars of the lattice, holding the wall against Vael with their own substance. When the keystone inverted, each god was sealed *into* its anchor. That is the Silence: the gods have spent 214 years bound at the bottom of their own sanctums. And it gets worse — when Forgeholm built the Hearts on the anchor sites, each god became the **wick**: the engines burn each kingdom's aspect *through* its god. Theora is being spent to keep Veritas silent. Tavan is being burned to keep Agoran warm. The extraction symptoms are god-shaped wounds.

**Amara, the sixth.** The god of Love — the "and" of the pantheon, never worshipped alone because Amara *was* the together. Amaranth is the god's own name hiding in plain sight (Amara's-land), the way Sophara is Sophar's. Amara held the keystone at activation and took the inversion's full force: unmade with the kingdom, erased from every liturgy — the missing verse is Amara's verse of the lullaby-hymn, the sixth wagon is Amara's traveling shrine (Love's temple had no fixed place; the god traveled as **the Guest**), and the Guest customs are five kingdoms' muscle memory of a god they cannot remember inviting. Whatever remains of Amara is the hum in the keystone and the warmth in the Loosening. The campaign-ending option of a "restored keystone of Love" ([Anchors & Engines — the Endgame](anchors-and-engines.md#the-endgame--the-keystone-problem)) is, theologically, the resurrection of a god — possibly *as* the community of Amaranth's descendants rather than as a person. Decide late; seed early.

**Korrven's apotheosis.** Read theologically, his endgame is explicit: a man making himself the sixth god. Re-tuning every anchor to himself and standing as living keystone means standing *where Amara stood* — becoming the binding, the god of a love that cannot leave. His occupation of Tavan's liturgy in Agoran is the rehearsal. This gives the campaign's final confrontation its true scale, and gives any cleric or seraph PC a personal stake: their silent god is a prisoner in the machine the king wants to seize.

**Anchors and gods at the table:**
- **Re-tuning an anchor wakes its god** — slowly, and *quietly*. Recommended: woken gods do not manifest avatars or armies; they answer the way aspects answer — a dream that's exact, a craft-insight, one true sentence in a sanctum after two centuries of nothing. The first prayer answered in 214 years should be a scene, not a stat block. A woken god visibly weakens Korrven's claim to godhood and enrages Vael.
- **Destroying an anchor releases its god maimed** — free, alive, and burned down to almost nothing, a god-shaped convalescent unable to help for a long time. This keeps the destroy option fast, honest, and costly, per [the anchor choice](anchors-and-engines.md#what-the-party-can-do-with-an-anchor).
- **Divine PCs:** a cleric/seraph of a silent god is the best religious hook in the setting — let their god be the *first* to speak. During the Loosening, the local god is nearest the surface; a divine PC on fairgrounds might hear something. Lean generous with Hope for acts of worship in the old style (two aspects braided), per the [Wayfair table notes](factions/the-wayfair.md#gm-notes).
- **Who knows the truth:** nobody living. The Wayfair's elders keep Amara's liturgy without the theology; Veritas's vaults hold pre-Sundering scripture of the Concord of Six — written in the [Concord Tongue](history.md#gm-notes), Amaranth's unmade language, so it cannot simply be read; the deepest confirmation is written in the Architect's notes sealed in Kaldrum's Guild archive. The reveal chain runs: Guest customs → sixth-god-shaped hole → *the gods are in the Hearts* → what the king is really building.

**Open questions (deliberate):** Did the gods *consent* knowingly to the risk of the Working, or were they deceived along with everyone else — and does any god know who the Architect was? Can a fully-burned god die, and does its aspect gutter worldwide if so (a possible doom-clock if the party stalls)? What does Amara's "resurrection" actually look like — person, place, or practice? Decide when the table forces the issue, not before.
