# Herrek, the Old Wright

**Role:** The king's father — alive, enslaved, and chief engineer of his son's apotheosis machine; the campaign's buried bombshell
**Location:** [The Foundation](../../locations/the-foundation.md) — the Housing's drawing-shed, and the Dig
**First appeared:** *(not yet — the Quiet Harvest, late; his name is spoken nowhere before then)*
**Status:** Alive. Nobody living knows this matters.

## Appearance

Somewhere past seventy, bent like a drawn bow, hands ruined and precise. Wears the same wrights' leathers he was exiled in, mended so many times the original cloth is a rumor. The work-gangs call him **the Old Wright**; the Steward's roll says **Harl**, which is what he gave them, and which was his own master's name sixty years ago. He looks at every machine the way other old men look at grandchildren. He has not spoken a wright's oath aloud in nineteen years, and mouths one, silently, before every shift anyway.

## Personality

Exact, dry, unbroken — a man who lost the verdict, the Guild, his family, and his name, and kept the one thing exile couldn't scour: the craft, and the ethic under it. *A tool is a promise to the hand that will use it* — he was formed by the carving everyone else stopped reading. He directs the Housing build because refusing kills the gangs assigned to him, and he does it *well*, because bad engineering kills them too, and he loathes the excellence of it with a completeness that keeps him alive. To the prisoners he is dour, demanding, and safe: gangs under the Old Wright do not die. They have noticed. Cade has noticed. Nobody asks why he cares, which is fortunate, because the answer is that everyone he ever failed to protect is dead, and these are what he has.

## Backstory

Senior wright of the Mother Engine; the man who traced the draw, found the anchor and the lattice beneath it, and attempted the splice — condemned in the sealed tribunal of 195 AS as a tamperer, scoured with his whole line ([the truth](the-king.md#gm-notes): the splice *took*). Exile scattered the family in the wilds; he searched two winters, found graves and worse, and stopped. He has believed his son dead for nineteen years. It is the only measurement he ever got wrong on purpose — the alternative did not bear weighing.

He survived sixteen years on the Thornfell fringes as a mending-hermit the moor-folk called the **Briar Wright**, fixing pumps and stills for people who never asked his history. The Gleaner's early sweeps took him in 211 — an old man with wright's hands and no kingdom's grip, flagged in an afternoon. At the Foundation they gave him drawings to check. He corrected them. It was that or watch the scaffolds fall on children from Sophara.

## Relationship to the Party

The prize at the bottom of the Quiet Harvest, though the party won't know it until they do. As "the Old Wright" he is a wary, invaluable ally inside the camp — the man who knows the Housing's every tolerance, the Dig's every ladder, and the March's moods better than Cade guesses. As *Herrek* he is the campaign's master key: the living proof of the tribunal's error, the one mind that fully understands the lattice, and the father of the man the world is learning to fear.

## Secrets

- **The second stage.** Hidden in the Housing's root, drawn in structure no other wright alive can read as anything but bracing: the unfinished half of his repair — the graft that closes the drain for *everyone*, fitted secretly into the engine meant to enslave it ([the Foundation](../../locations/the-foundation.md#key-features)). He needs the Guild's craft and every kingdom's touch to finish it. He believes he will die before that sentence means anything.
- **He does not know the king's name.** Prisoners know only *the king*. The hooded man who walked the Dig passed ten feet from him; Herrek marked the visitor's wright-trained gait and thought, with professional approval and no recognition, *someone taught him to walk a site.*
- **He carries the Architect's inheritance** — the family notes, memorized whole before the tribunal took the papers ([the bloodline](the-king.md#gm-notes)). The eldest-to-eldest secret died with his exile, untold: his son was never given it. He believes this is the one good thing his ruin accomplished.
- **The hum knows him.** At the Dig's bottom, the keystone's note changes — slightly, measurably, only for him. He has measured it. He has told no one. The lattice answers the blood that corrupted it, and he is the last man alive who knows *why* that is terrifying.

## GM Notes

**Guard the name — both directions.** The reveal is a matched pair and the campaign's heaviest scene either way it lands: Herrek learning the king is *Korrven* (the son he buried, alive, crowned, and monstrous — grief and joy arriving in the same breath), or Korrven learning the Old Wright is *Herrek* (the grievance that built him, standing alive in his own camp — every story Vael ever curated for him detonating at once). Do not spend either cheaply. The strongest staging gives the party the knowledge first and makes *them* decide who learns, and when, and how gently.

**What his rescue arms.** Everything: the tribunal truth with a living voice (Vorn's re-measurement, [the centerpiece defense](../../world/kingdoms/forgeholm.md#gm-notes), now has its witness); the second-stage repair (the alternative route to the anchors that bypasses Korrven entirely); the Vael-lever (the bond's proof of concealment — *it knew your father lived*, [the sharpest single lever](../../locations/the-foundation.md#gm-notes)); and the father himself, the one person whose word about the exile Korrven cannot dismiss. A live Herrek at the endgame turns *kill the king vs. free the man* from a debate into a family matter.

**The Ondrel thread.** [Ondrel Vasht](../../world/factions/the-wayfair.md#key-members) was a journeyman at the tribunal — the one living outsider who could look at the Old Wright and say the name. If the fair ever reaches the March's edge, or a rescued Herrek reaches the fair, that recognition scene plays itself.

**His death, if it comes.** If Herrek dies at the Foundation — the Harvest ignored too long, the breakout too late — he dies with the second stage unexplained and the name unspoken, and the campaign gets crueler: the repair becomes an archaeology problem, and Korrven never gets the one truth with hands. Let the table feel the clock, but if it happens, give him the wrights' end: mid-shift, holding a load until the gang is clear.
