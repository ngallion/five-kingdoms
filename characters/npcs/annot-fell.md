# Annot Fell

**Role:** Journeyman copyist — the hand that routes [the Marginalia](../../world/factions/the-marginalia.md); the party's way into the Archive, and to the Last Reader
**Location:** The bindery by day, the Stacks' running-stairs the rest of the time, [Meridian](../../locations/meridian.md); a room in the Hem she mostly uses for sleeping
**First appeared:** *(not yet — planned for the Veritas arc)*
**Status:** Carrying the whole network in her head, the Deep Stacks' suppers down the stairs, and one secret too many

## Appearance

Mid-twenties, Hem-born and it shows: quick, warm-faced, dressed in copyist's grey with ink to both elbows by noon. Moves through the Stacks at the porter's half-run that ranked scholars find faintly scandalous. Her own hand — the thing that would astonish the Archive if the Archive ever looked — is beautiful: she can write six scripts, imitate any hand in the building, and signs nothing.

## Personality

The warmest person in the coldest kingdom, and painfully aware of the mismatch. Annot talks the way Veritans don't — easily, generously, in questions — but she learned early to ration it above the Hem: up in the Lines, her friendliness reads as unranked presumption. So she pours it into the margins instead. Her penciled questions are the best in the Archive — precise, open-hearted, incapable of malice — and nobody knows they are hers, which is the shape of her whole life: everywhere, load-bearing, and invisible.

## Backstory

Tansy Mor's grand-niece, raised in the grain-halls where talk is free, apprenticed to the bindery at eleven because her letters were too fine to waste on sacking-labels. The Marginalia found her — or she found it — the way every margin-hand does: she answered something. By twenty she was the network's memory; by now she *is* the routing — which book travels which chain, which hands are in dialogue, and (the dangerous part) **whose hands they are**. Ten years of copying has given her the one skill the network cannot survive existing: she can identify any annotator in the Archive by a half-line of pencil. The anonymity that protects two hundred people is, in practice, one young woman deciding every day not to know what she knows.

And for ten years she has carried supper down to [Edda Prine](edda-prine.md) — a Stacks-crew chore nobody else wanted. Somewhere in the third year the old woman started saying one dry sentence a week to her. Annot would not call it friendship out loud, in case naming it broke it. It is the closest thing either of them has.

## Relationship to the Party

The door. Every path into the arc runs through what Annot can do: reach Prine (nobody else can), route a message through the Marginalia, name what the Stacks-crews have seen (which vaults [Marr](ansel-marr.md) has asked after, which towers went dark, where the First Shelves' crates are being built), or — the big one — raise the network for [the Reading](../../locations/meridian.md#gm-notes). She will like the party quickly; liking people is her nature and nobody in Veritas has let her practice it. But every favor she does is her deciding, again, that these strangers are worth the two hundred people she could unmask by being wrong.

## Secrets

- **She knows who went silent.** Doctor Hess Ulme's forty-year counterpart — the other hand of the theorem — is on the Concordance's northbound rolls; she identified the hand within a week of the last annotation. Telling Ulme breaks the network's one absolute custom and might break the man; not telling him is breaking him slowly anyway. She has carried the choice alone for a season. It is the first thing she will ever ask anyone for help with, if anyone earns it.
- **She has seen the rot firsthand.** She copies Concord glyphs for practice — the bindery repairs those crumbling covers too — and knows, personally, that her perfect copies come apart in her memory by morning while every other script she has ever learned stays. She has no theory. She has an exact, private, load-bearing observation that the oldest language in the vaults is *kept* dead — which makes her one of perhaps three people alive holding direct evidence of the unmaking, and the only one who tells jokes.
- **Marr has never written to her.** Two years of the Correspondent answering everyone, and the woman who knows more about the Archive than the Catalogue does has never received a letter — because copyists aren't in the Catalogue. She noticed. It is the only bitterness she owns, it is exactly the wound his method feeds on, and the day he realizes his oversight and writes to her — accurate, kind, *naming her questions from the margins as hers* — is the most dangerous letter in the arc. *(GM: hold this in reserve. He identifies her hand the same way she identifies others'; it is only a matter of time.)*

## GM Notes

**She is the arc's conscience, not its key — play the difference.** Mechanically she can open almost everything, and the temptation is to use her as a lockpick. Resist it: every use of Annot should cost a *decision*, hers or the party's — unmask a hand, break a custom, spend ten years of trust. If the party treats her as infrastructure, let her notice; the moment she goes quiet and formal with them, they have lost the arc's heart and should feel it.

**The Ulme question is her hinge scene.** Whatever the party advises — tell him, don't, or bring him in and let the network decide together — teaches her (and the table) what this campaign thinks fellowship is *for*. Handled with care, it recruits Ulme, seasons the network for the Reading, and gives Annot the thing she has never had: co-conspirators. Handled carelessly, it is the early unmasking that scatters the Marginalia a season before they are needed.

**At the Reading, she is the register.** The translation lives only in the people who hear it together — and the network's whole craft is holding knowledge in many hands. Annot's part in the climax is to do openly, once, the thing she has done secretly her whole life: stand in a room and know exactly who every hand is, out loud, by choice. First appearance of her signature on anything. Let it land.

**If the arc fails,** she survives it — Hem-tough, and too far beneath notice to purge — and becomes the campaign's saddest ongoing asset: the last index of a shipped-away library, feeding the party shelf-marks for vaults that now stand in Commonhold. She would still bring Prine's supper down, the long winter the old woman is dying. Somebody should.
