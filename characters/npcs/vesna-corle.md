# Vesna Corle, the Arbiter of Record

**Role:** Presiding voice of the Assay, keeper of the Great Catalogue — ruler of Veritas in everything but title; obstacle, then hinge
**Location:** The Eye's east wing and a tower at E0 by N1, [Meridian](../../locations/meridian.md) — one block off Zero, the best address in the kingdom
**First appeared:** *(not yet — planned for the Veritas arc)*
**Status:** Twenty-six years in the seat; being courted, correctly and patiently, by the king's Correspondent

## Appearance

Seventies, straight as a survey line, dressed in archive-grey with no ornament but the Catalogue key on its chain. Speaks rarely, never repeats herself, and is never misquoted — people remember her sentences the way they remember burns. Writes a hand so exact the bindery uses it to calibrate apprentices. Her one tell: when something genuinely interests her, she goes *more* still.

## Personality

The doctrine of silence made flesh — and its most disciplined believer. Corle is not cold by nature; she is cold by *conclusion*, and the conclusion was honestly earned ([see Secrets](#secrets)). She believes sharing ruins knowledge and ruins the scholar, believes it with the conviction of someone who has run the experiment, and has spent twenty-six years building the kingdom's procedures into a shape where no one need ever be ruined again. She is, by her own lights, protecting every scholar in Veritas from the worst day of her life.

She is also the best-informed person in the kingdom, because the Catalogue passes under her eye — and the loneliest, for the same reason. She has read more of Veritas's sealed knowledge than anyone alive is supposed to, one entry at a time, and has no one to be wrong in front of.

## Backstory

Corle was young once, and loud. Her early masterwork — a resonance survey still cited by seal-number across three faculties — was written *with a partner*: **Doro Ashwin**, the other half of a two-year collaboration conducted out loud, at one table, in open violation of every custom of their caste. It was the best work either ever did, and it nearly killed her doing it — with every session she felt her own grasp of their subject dim and blur, insight pouring away as fast as the two of them built it *(the Silent Heart, drinking a shared discovery at the source — but she has never heard of the Silent Heart)*. She concluded the obvious, wrong thing: that collaboration is a fire that burns the collaborators. She sealed the work under her name and Ashwin's, ended the partnership in one conversation she has never repeated to anyone, and built the rest of her life as the wall against that fire.

Ashwin argued, lost, and left — one more Veritan scholar who talked too much, gone to the [Wayfair](../../world/factions/the-wayfair.md), where Veritans teach freely and feel enriched by it. Corle does not know that last part. It is the single most important fact in the kingdom she rules.

## Relationship to the Party

The gate. The First Shelves open lawfully only with her key; the Assay follows her stillness the way the Colloquy follows Meren's voice; and any party plan that runs through the Archive runs through her. Played straight she is an obstacle of the best kind — incorruptible, consistent, and *right by her own evidence*. She will receive the party exactly once, listen with total attention, and rule against them in one sentence. Getting a second audience is the arc.

## Secrets

- **The notebook.** She kept the working notebook of the Ashwin collaboration — two hands alternating on every page, the record of the only fellowship of her life. It is in her private vault, catalogued under a false seal, and she reads it on the nights the noon bell frays. She has never once admitted, even inwardly, that what she feels reading it is not regret but *homesickness*.
- **She has noticed the Concordance's arithmetic.** Priority claims from northbound scholars clear the Assay in days instead of years; she has said nothing, because the fast-tracked seals are procedurally impeccable and the alternative — that her own tribunal's currency is being debased under her seal — is unthinkable. She is one honest ledger away from being the Correspondent's most dangerous enemy.
- **Marr's offer is aimed exactly.** Not flattery, not rank: **the Catalogue, completed.** Every archive on the plateau unified, cross-indexed, finished — her life's work, done, in Commonhold's Concordance Hall. She has refused twice. She has kept the letters.

## GM Notes

**The turn.** Corle cannot be argued into opening the Deep Stacks; she can only be *shown* — her doctrine is empirical, so the counter must be evidence. Three exhibits, in rising order: Bellkeep Vim's drawer (the world's machinery has changed and Veritas is measuring it in secret); the Concordance ledger (her tribunal's seals, spent as the king's coin); and the killing one — **Doro Ashwin, alive, sharp, and generous**, stepping off a Wayfair wagon having taught for thirty years without burning. Her whole life is built on the premise that the fire was the price of the table. Show her an old woman who kept the table and was never burned — because the fair's braid slips the Heart's grip — and the wall she built has nothing left to stand on. Whether that breaks her or frees her is the best scene in the arc; write it at the table, not before.

**Turned, she is the arc's heaviest piece.** The lawful key to the First Shelves; the Assay delivered in a single sitting; and at [the Reading](../../locations/meridian.md#gm-notes) itself, a place held open — the Arbiter of Record, keeper of the kingdom's every seal, choosing to *listen* to the first spoken truth in two centuries. Her presence makes the translation official in the only sense Veritas understands: witnessed.

**If she is never turned:** she seals the Deep Stacks against all parties — the king's and the players' alike — with total procedural correctness, and the corpus survives crated in her vaults instead of Commonhold's: the one failure mode where Vael half-wins *through* an honest woman. Leave her that dignity; she is not a villain. She is what the kingdom did to its best student.
