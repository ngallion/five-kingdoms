# Ansel Marr, the Correspondent

**Role:** Korrven's agent in Veritas — Tier 2 lieutenant; director of the Concordance and the first audience the kingdom has had in living memory
**Location:** A study at E2 by S5, [Meridian](../../locations/meridian.md) — lamplit past the Dimming, by special dispensation nobody remembers granting
**First appeared:** *(not yet — planned for the Veritas arc)*
**Status:** Embedded two years; the want-list is half filled, and the Last Reader has kept his letters

## Appearance

Fifties, rumpled in a way that reads as scholarly rather than careless, ink on the right cuff always. A Veritan face — plateau-weathered, squint-lined — because it is one: he is native, which no other lieutenant can say of their kingdom. His habit, remarked by everyone: he answers *every* letter, in a hand people keep — precise, warm, unhurried, three days at most. Being read by Ansel Marr, scholars say, is like being believed. That is the design.

## Personality

Patient, genuinely learned, and moved by other people's work the way other men are moved by music — his praise is accurate, his questions are better than most scholars' theses, and his gratitude to the king is the engine under all of it. Marr is one of Korrven's grateful ([His Army](the-king.md#his-army)): a man the kingdom's machinery destroyed with total procedural correctness, whom the king found and gave the only role that ever fit — *the one who answers*. He believes, wholly, that he is saving Veritas's scholars from a kingdom that buries them alive in their own vaults. He is not entirely wrong, which is what makes him dangerous.

He never recruits at a sealing. He recruits at the *silence after* — the week when a scholar has vaulted a life's work, performed the Silent Witness on the Eye's steps, and walks home through the Dimming to a tower where no one will ever ask about it. His letter arrives that week. It asks.

## Backstory

Born in the Lines, ranked young, and jumped: his life's discovery — twenty years on the resonance of paired instruments — reached the Claim-Vault three days after a rival's seal. Three days. The rival's claim was thinner, later-conceived, and (Marr is certain, and is right) backdated with a bribed witness — but seals are compared, never contents, and the Assay ruled in one sentence. He appealed; the appeal was itself ruled a slander of the seals; he was **unranked** — struck from the Catalogue, the Veritan death. He walked the Screes road east with the other ghosts, and somewhere past the last waystation the [Gleaner's](../../world/factions/the-found.md) people found him, as they find everyone the kingdoms throw away.

He came home in 212 AS as a corresponding scholar "of the border academies," with Agoran grain-money behind a bottomless postal budget, and built the kingdom's harvest in three tiers:

- **The letters** — the base of everything: two years of answering every scholar who writes, until half the Lines would defend him unasked. His archive of correspondence is incidentally the finest intelligence map of Veritas ever assembled — every scholar's work, wound, and want, filed in their own hand.
- **The Concordance** — the pipeline: the king's universal catalogue, and the Corresponding Fellowship that staffs it. Your work, sent north, is read, answered, cited, *used* — and it is; [the offer delivers](the-king.md). The letters home rhyme. The towers go dark one by one, and the priority claims of Fellowship scholars clear the Assay in days *(he has two of the nine assayers answering his letters; they do not know what they are part of)*.
- **The want-list** — the masterstroke he did not write *(see Secrets)*.

His standing orders mirror the other lieutenants' in kind: don't break Veritas — *harvest* it, and ready the invitation. The kingdom must ask the king in.

## Relationship to the Party

Generous. He will have read whatever trail the party leaves — reports, letters, a Wayfair bill, testimony from Sophara — and will discuss their own deeds with them more insightfully than their friends do. PCs with a scholar's soul — or a scholar's wound, the ache to be *understood* — get the full treatment: he is Korrven's method retail, the exact right reader at the exact unread moment. And he does the party genuine favors for as long as they stir the kingdom up, because a Veritas in ferment writes more letters.

## Secrets

- **The want-list.** Folded into his orders came a list in a hand that is not the king's — he has compared; he keeps everything — naming the Deep Stacks' oldest holdings: located, crated, shipped north **sealed, unread**. He has never been given a reason, and the clause grinds against everything he believes his king to be. A king who reads — who is the *proof* that being read matters — ordering the oldest library in the world kept shut? Marr has decided not to think about it, and thinks about it constantly. *(GM truth: the hand is the question. Whether Vael's curation arrives through some hollowed amanuensis in Commonhold or by a stranger mechanism is yours to decide — but the clause is Vael's, not Korrven's, and it is the visible seam between the king and his god. See [Veritas — GM Notes](../../world/kingdoms/veritas.md#gm-notes).)*
- **He has identified the bribed witness.** Four months of his own files gave him the man who falsified the seal that unmade his life — old now, ranked, comfortable. Marr has done nothing. His grievance is *load-bearing*: the king's version of his story requires the kingdom to be the villain, and taking private revenge would shrink it to one crooked clerk. Some nights he understands this about himself. Those are bad nights.
- **The Assay is half his.** Two assayers correspond with him devotedly; a third keeps his letters and hasn't answered yet. If it comes to a ruling — on the party, on the fair, on opening the First Shelves — he can swing procedure without ever appearing in the record.
- **Prine is the one he writes by hand.** Every other correspondence is copied fair by his clerks. Hers he drafts twice and copies himself, and he has caught himself *wanting her to refuse* — because the day she accepts, the finest reader he has ever known pours out her life's understanding and goes dark, and he will have been the ear that did it. He files this feeling under professional thoroughness. It is not that.

## GM Notes

**The unmasking must beat the answering.** Marr's position is perfectly legal and genuinely beloved — the man who reads, in the kingdom nobody reads. Exposing him is not a matter of proof but of *procedure*: the Concordance ledger (the rhyming letters, the darkened towers, the fast-tracked seals) must be laid before the Assay by its own rules, seal-numbers and dates, because Veritas cannot hear any other language. [Vesna Corle](vesna-corle.md) is the hinge — her tribunal's debased currency is the one wound that outranks her doctrine. Done clumsily, Marr simply produces his correspondence — thousands of letters of real, documented kindness — and the party plays the outsiders who attacked the only man who ever answered.

**The turn runs through the clause.** Harder than Maro, easier than Pitch: his faith is real but his *grievance* is honest, and honest grievances can be re-aimed. The crack is the want-list. Marr is a reader ordered to keep something unread, and he knows in his marrow that the order is wrong — he just believes it must be the king's. Show him it isn't: the party that comes out of [the Reading](../../locations/meridian.md#gm-notes) can tell him what the sealed crates *say* — including what Vael has kept from Korrven himself — and Marr faces the exact shape of his own story again: a truth buried by procedure, a man unmade by a sealed ruling. The king he serves is being jumped the way Marr was jumped. A turned Correspondent delivers the entire Concordance pipeline, the want-list's provenance, two assayers, and the finest intelligence archive in five kingdoms — and the Gleaner's people will come for him within the season, which he will know before the party does. Sheltering a defected Correspondent is a quest in itself.

**The clock.** If the party never engages him, the crates go north through the winter and Prine accepts in the spring — exhaustion and kindness, not corruption. Warning signs available early: towers dark in a pattern along the Lines; letters home that rhyme; Annot Fell mentioning that the Stacks-crews have been asked, twice, politely, for the shelf-marks of the First Shelves.
