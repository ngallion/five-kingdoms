# Korrven

**Role:** Primary villain, conquering king
**Based in:** Agoran (the Kingdom of Community)
**Status:** Alive, rising in power — currently eyeing Forgeholm

## The Core of Him

He was exiled — cast out of Forgeholm as a young man, weeks from taking his own wright's marks, by the very Guild he had spent his life earning his way into. Not for anything he did: for his father's name. A sealed tribunal ruled his father, **Wright Herrek** of the Mother Engine, a tamperer — and Guild law scours the whole line. Korrven was never told what his father actually stood accused of. He believes, with absolute conviction, that Herrek's only crime was trying to make the Hearts *better* — that Forgeholm destroyed a man for trying to mend the thing it loved most, then threw his family into the wilds after him. *(What really happened: see GM Notes.)*

That wound never healed — it became a furnace. He spent years in the margins, finding others like him: misfits, monsters, cast-outs. He gave them what he always wanted. A home. A place where they belonged. Agoran — hollowed out and starving for exactly that — welcomed them all, and made him a king.

And now he is going home.

He does not rule through fear alone. He gives people something real — purpose, identity, a place to belong. The people of Agoran are genuinely grateful to him. That is the horror of it.

## His Power

*Korrven's power flows from his bond with **[Vael, the Unmade](../../world/vael-the-unmade.md)** — an entity of primordial loneliness older than the five kingdoms. The bond is not possession. It is alignment. Their goals are the same.*

### He Feeds on Isolation

Every sundering between people — every exile, every closed border, every kingdom that turns inward — he feels it and grows stronger. The five kingdoms' centuries of isolation have been feeding him, slowly, for a long time. He may not have caused their isolation, but he has learned to cultivate it.

When the kingdoms draw apart, he grows. When they come together, he weakens. The heroes uniting the kingdoms is not just strategy — it is the only thing that can actually kill him. Violence alone won't do it.

### He Knows the Wound

He has an uncanny, almost preternatural sense for what people lack — what a kingdom has been starved of, what an individual is desperate for. This is not manipulation exactly. He is genuinely perceptive, genuinely empathetic in a dark way. He *understands* loneliness because he lived it.

When he approaches a kingdom, he offers exactly what it needs. His offer is always real. His version is always poisoned.

- **To Luminar** he offers meaning — *your creations will serve a great purpose, my purpose, and they will last forever*
- **To Veritas** he offers collaboration — *your discoveries will be shared, studied, built upon — by my scholars, in my service*
- **To Forgeholm** he offers direction — *tell me what to build and I will tell you why it matters; your machines will change the world*
- **To Sophara** he offers action — *you know what is right; I will give you the power to make it so*
- **To Agoran** he offered belonging — *you were empty; here is meaning; here is purpose; here is an enemy that explains your suffering*

The insidious part: he delivers. Luminar's art under his reign *does* have purpose. Agoran *does* have meaning now. It just comes with chains nobody can see.

### The Cycle

His poisoned gifts deepen the isolation in a new way — kingdoms that take his offer close off from the others on his behalf. This feeds him further. He is not just a conqueror. He is a system.

## What He Wants

- A community he controls completely — one that cannot exile him, because he *is* the community
- Forgeholm's technology, bent to his will — direction without wisdom is exactly what a tyrant needs
- Eventually: all five aspects under one crown, and with them, every dimension of human life

## His Army

Misfits, outcasts, and monsters — people who had nothing before he found them. He gave them purpose when the world gave them nothing. They will die for him, and many of them are not bad people. That is the point. His army is not evil, it is *grateful*. Fighting them is not clean.

Its public face abroad is **the King's Wardens** — the grey-cloaked companies who ride against the rising wilds in every kingdom, free of charge, under the motto *No one stands alone*. They are heroes, and most of them believe every word of it. What a quiet few of them do between rescues is the campaign's other great secret: see [The Hollowing](../../world/the-hollowing.md).

## Relationship to the Party

He will try to do to the party what he has done to every kingdom: find the wound, offer the cure. He will approach each hero with the thing they most want. He may even be *right* about what they need. He is just the wrong person to give it.

## GM Notes

**His endgame (see [The Anchors & the Hearts](../../world/anchors-and-engines.md)):** He is not collecting kingdoms — he is collecting *anchors*. His true goal is to re-run the Anchor Working with himself as the living keystone: every kingdom bound permanently to him, a community that cannot exile its king because he *is* the binding. Forgeholm is next because its Enginewrights' Guild holds the only craft that touches the anchors.

**His army's secret:** Many of his misfits and outcasts are descendants of **Amaranth**, the unmade sixth kingdom — the heirs of Love, aspectless in a sorted world, marching for the thing that erased them. They may also be the key to the campaign's best ending (see history.md GM Notes).

**His origin — the truth of the exile (195 AS):** Herrek was one of the senior wrights who noticed what the Hearts actually burn ([Who Knows What](../../world/anchors-and-engines.md#who-knows-what-gm-reference)). Quiet retirement wasn't in him. He traced the draw beneath the Mother Engine, found the anchor — and beneath the anchor, the lattice, which he alone had the notes to recognize (see *The bloodline*, below) — and attempted a splice: the first stage of a repair meant to close the drain. The Guild caught him at the work, found what looked like a dead graft fused into the engine's root, ruled it *failed* sabotage, sealed the tribunal, and exiled his line. **The Guild was wrong. The splice took.** Herrek succeeded in tying the Mother Engine into the lattice itself — his "failure" is the reason the Mother Engine is the master tap on every anchor. He almost certainly died in exile believing the Guild's verdict. *(Option: he didn't — a living Herrek, in the Hollow March or among the Wayfair, is a late-campaign bombshell.)*

**The bloodline (deepest layer):** Herrek and Korrven descend from **the Architect** — Vael's first partner, the designer whose "corrected" keystone inverted the Working and unmade Amaranth ([History — GM Notes](../../world/history.md#gm-notes)). The family carried the Architect's notes and shame in secret, eldest to eldest; Herrek's splice was atonement, built from his ancestor's own designs, and it took *because* the lattice recognizes the blood that corrupted it. The tribunal came before he ever passed the secret to his son. So Vael's first and second partners are one line — it never "found" Korrven; it came back for a family it has been tending for two centuries. Korrven suffers from a wound his own ancestor made, wields a power keyed to his own blood, and knows neither. Three generations, one lattice: the Architect corrupted the binding, Herrek died trying to mend it, Korrven means to *become* it.

**The irony that runs the campaign:** Korrven's endgame — seize the Guild, re-tune every anchor through the Mother Engine — runs entirely on his father's secret success. The master tap he is coming to take *is Herrek's work*, and he has no idea. And Vael knows. It felt the splice take, and the exile that followed rang straight down the freshly tied lattice — that is why the wound "rang like a struck bell" ([Vael](../../world/vael-the-unmade.md#how-it-found-korrven)). It has never told him: the grievance as Korrven tells it — a visionary punished for loving the work — is better fuel than the truth.

**The truth as a weapon:** the sealed tribunal record still sits in the Guild's deep archive ([Forgeholm](../../world/kingdoms/forgeholm.md#gm-notes)). Herrek's splice was an act of *repair* — reconnection, the drain closed for everyone. His son inherited the wound and inverted the answer: where the father tried to fix the binding, the son means to *become* it. A party that proves this to Korrven holds one of the few levers that reaches him — vindication and indictment on the same page, plus proof that Vael curates what he knows. Whether it cracks him open or hardens him is a table-defining scene, and it feeds directly into the "can he be saved" question in the Vael file. *(Mechanical option: Herrek's splice had a second stage he never finished. Completing his repair — Guild craft plus something of every kingdom — is an alternative route to the anchors that doesn't pass through Korrven at all.)*

**The shepherd and the wolves (see [The Hollowing](../../world/the-hollowing.md)):** the monster surge is his second system, running in parallel with the poisoned offers. The March breathes the things out because *his own re-tuning* strains the lattice; they hunt the isolated because they share Vael's appetite; his Dousers aim the tide by carving channels of cut connection; and his Wardens then save the survivors, gloriously. He manufactures the fear, sells the shelter, and both halves are real. Note the self-feeding loop: the strain he causes breeds the monsters that close the roads that deepen the isolation that feeds him. Proof of the herding is a reveal on par with the tribunal record — and unlike the tribunal, this one indicts him with every kingdom at once.

**The tragic read:** He is solving the same problem the heroes are — he just chose domination instead of cooperation. He saw that the five kingdoms needed each other and decided the solution was one king over all of them, not five equal partners. He is not wrong about the problem. He is catastrophically wrong about the solution.

**The irony of Agoran:** The Kingdom of Community, hollowed out by isolation, became the perfect vessel for him. He didn't corrupt it — it was already empty. He just filled it.

**The question the campaign asks:** Can something that was filled with poison be emptied and refilled? Can Agoran be healed, or just defeated?
