# Dorel Vane, the Teller

**Role:** The Luminary — ruler of Luminar (insofar as it has one); teller of the Undertide; the arc's exhausted hinge
**Location:** The Glasshouse, [Prismere](../../locations/prismere.md)
**First appeared:** *(not yet — planned for the Luminar arc)*
**Status:** Crowned three years and running dry — the king's finale is in his desk, unsigned

## Appearance

Late forties, beautiful the way a lit stage is beautiful — from the house. Up close: grey under the paint, a voice husbanded like the last of the lamp-oil, hands that shake until the curtain rises and then don't. Dresses each night in the same storm-blue coat, the one tradition Luminar has allowed in two hundred years, because it is *his* and the Undertide is the exception to every rule.

## Personality

A genuinely great storyteller wearing a crown that was meant to be temporary. Vane is vain, warm, evasive, and far more intelligent than the Bill gives him credit for — he understood years before anyone that what he had accidentally built was not a long premiere but a *shared story*, the first in living memory, and the understanding terrifies him. Every night he watches fishers and courtiers lean forward at the same line and knows he is holding something no Luminary ever held: an audience that *stays*. He also knows he is out of story, and that the two facts are on a collision course.

He deflects with charm, agrees with everyone, and decides nothing. It is not weakness exactly. A man carrying a lamp through a powder-house learns to move very, very slowly.

## Backstory

His crowning was an accident of desperation. Three years ago he debuted what he announced as a finished tale "too long for one night" — the story of the mirror-city under Prismere's water — intending to stretch a thin piece over a week. The city leaned in. He kept going. The Gasp came on the ninth night, and by the time he understood what he had — a work that could never fade *because it never finished* — he was Luminary, and the Undertide was the kingdom's nightly ritual, and there was no way off the stage that didn't end with the story dying and everyone forgetting why it mattered.

Three years of nightly invention have scraped him hollow. He borrowed — money for spectacle, collaborators for ideas — and the debts consolidated, gradually and then suddenly, in one gracious pair of hands. Last spring [Marlow Pitch](marlow-pitch.md) forgave the interest and made a gift of a manuscript: a finale, complete, magnificent, in which the drowned city rises at the call of a crowned stranger from the east who gathers the lost. Vane read it in one sitting and has not slept properly since. He has not said yes. He has stopped saying no.

## Relationship to the Party

The gate to the Glasshouse, the stage, and the finale — and a man looking, with increasing openness, for anyone who can hand him an ending he could bear to tell. He will charm the party, dodge them, test them with story-problems disguised as small talk, and — if they show him they understand what the Undertide actually *is* — come to them at night, coatless, and ask the question he cannot ask anyone in Luminar: *how does it end?*

## Secrets

- **He knows the finale is a weapon.** Not the mechanism — but a teller of his caliber can read what the manuscript *does*: it takes everything the city loves about the story and hands it to the stranger-king. He has told himself it is only patronage politics. He has noticed himself telling himself that.
- **The dreams.** Since the second year he dreams most nights of standing in the Understage before a wall of doors, all of them straining inward, something on the far side breathing in time with the tide. *(The Heart, three years starved of its meal, presses on the man withholding it — [Prismere GM Notes](../../locations/prismere.md#gm-notes).)* He has started paying Odile Strake small kindnesses to avoid going below the stage himself.
- **Where the Undertide comes from.** He no longer fully believes he is inventing it. Lines arrive whole; the mirror-city's geography stays consistent in ways his notes can't explain; once, a character wept over a word Vane didn't know, and a Veritan in the audience went white. Whether the serial is invention, or something drowned and erased telling itself through the one open channel it could find, is yours to decide — and is best left unresolved until the finale forces it.
- The storm-blue coat was his father's — a Flats bargeman who told stories on the night-watch and died unremembered. The Undertide's best lines are his. Vane has never said so from the stage; the Roll of Makers ([the Encore](../../world/factions/the-encore.md)) has no Vane the elder on it, and the omission is the guilt under the crown.

## GM Notes

**His hinge is an ending worth telling.** He cannot be bullied, bought (Pitch owns that market), or flattered onto the party's side — he can only be *out-authored*. Bring him a true last act: the history the party has actually uncovered — a drowned kingdom, an erased people, a city that rises not for a crowned stranger but *when everyone calls it together* — and watch the teller recognize a better story ([the counter-finale](../../locations/prismere.md#gm-notes)). His conversion scene is the mirror of Vorn's *bring me a number* and Meren's *bring her the finding*: **bring him the ending.** Once he has it, he is fearless; the man shakes offstage, never on.

**The father is the door.** If charm holds the party at arm's length, the coat is the crack: the first person to ask *who told stories to you?* meets the real Vane. A party that puts Vane the elder on the Roll of Makers — a bargeman remembered by name in front of his son — has done more to win the Luminary than any argument. Fold it into the finale if you can bear the table crying: the Undertide's last line, credited at last.

**If he tells Pitch's finale:** play it as tragedy, not betrayal — a drowning man handed the only rope in sight. He delivers it magnificently, the Gasp comes, [the kingdom falls the quiet way](../../world/kingdoms/luminar.md#gm-notes) — and the Heart takes the serial whole, which means it takes *him*: the teller wakes unable to remember why his life's work mattered, the storm-blue coat in his hands like a stranger's. If the party liberates Luminar later, a hollowed Vane telling nursery rhymes to the Flats' children for coins is the cost made visible — and teaching him the Undertide back out of Pell's memory is the redemption scene the Encore exists for.
