# First Steward Iveth Kall

**Role:** Keeper of the Hall of Names — mistress of Agoran's rolls, feasts, and Setting-Outs; the hinge of Commonhold
**Location:** The Hall of Names, [Commonhold](../../locations/commonhold.md)
**First appeared:** *(not yet — the first face of authority any visitor to Commonhold meets)*
**Status:** Alive, indispensable, and keeping one name off one list

## Appearance

Late forties, broad and capable, flour on her sleeves as often as ink — she cooks at the feasts she administers. Wears no badge of office; in Agoran, being known *is* the badge, and everyone knows Iveth. Her voice was trained young for the moot-grace and it carries: when she sings the blessing over the Ring, a thousand people breathe together without deciding to.

## Personality

Warmth with a spine of iron filing-cards. Iveth is genuinely kind, endlessly organized, and constitutionally unable to let a person go unfed, unnamed, or unvisited — the rolls of belonging are not her job but her nature, given a kingdom to work with. She remembers naming-days by the hundred, sends stew to sickbeds, and can tell a table's troubles from how it sounds at supper. She is also, without one flicker of malice, the most effective intelligence apparatus in the five kingdoms: everything the Hall of Names learns by loving attention, the [quiet corps](../../world/factions/the-found.md#how-they-operate) reads by lamplight, and Iveth has decided not to wonder what they do with it.

## Backstory

Commonhold-born, prentice grace-singer to [Oren Brandt](oren-brandt.md), the last moot-speaker — he trained her voice, taught her every verse, and expected her to succeed him at the Ring. What she never told him: she sang thirty years of the old grace and felt *nothing come back*. Her whole childhood the tables were full and hollow — feasts everyone attended and no one meant, warmth performed over an emptiness nobody could name ([the extraction](../../world/anchors-and-engines.md#agoran--the-warm-heart-now-the-kings-heart), though she has no such word). She believed the fault was hers, that a grace-singer who felt nothing was a fraud in the god's own house.

Then the king came, and the flame rose, and the warmth *worked*. Whatever her teacher walked out over, Iveth knows what she felt at the first feast of the new grace: the thing the old words had promised her whole life and never once delivered. She changed one name in the liturgy Oren taught her, and tells herself nothing else changed. She has served the King's Table since, and her gratitude — like all the [Found](../../world/factions/the-found.md)'s — is real, and earned, and aimed at the wrong cause.

## Relationship to the Party

The welcome itself. Visitors to Commonhold are Iveth's guests within the day — fed, named, entered on a roll, invited to the feast. She will like the party sincerely, notice everything about them, and be the reason the king knows their wounds by the week's end. She is also the best honest window into fallen Agoran: ask her *why* she serves and she will tell the truth — the hollow years, the flame rising, the warmth that finally came back — and the party should find they cannot argue with her experience, only with its explanation.

## Secrets

- **The unstruck name.** Oren Brandt walked out rather than sing the king's grace, and by law his name should have been struck. Iveth keeps the roll of the old Ring-table herself, and in five years has never struck it. His plate is entered as *traveling*. Nobody audits the First Steward. It is the only dishonest line in forty thousand of her entries, and she thinks about it every time the flame gutters.
- **She performs the Setting-Outs.** Someone must sing the unseating, and she will not delegate a cruelty. She weeps at every one, openly — Agorans find this fitting, the sorrow that proves the sentence's weight — and she has begun keeping a private count of how many of the unseated were people the quiet corps had asked her about first. The count is not random. She has stopped adding to it, which is not the same as forgetting it.
- **The flame-log.** Her private log of the Common Hearth's bad nights ([the seismograph](../../locations/commonhold.md#gm-notes)) — kept because a grace-singer watches the fire. She has noticed the gutterings cluster when certain things happen far away. She has told no one, because the last person who felt something wrong in the warmth was her teacher, and she knows how that ended.

## Daggerheart Stats

*(Tier 2-equivalent — social. She should never be fought; she should be **answered**. Move ideas: **The Guest's Portion** — a PC accepts her hospitality; the GM gains a Fear usable only to reveal something the Hall has learned about them; **A Thousand Breathing Together** — when she sings at the Ring, crowds act as one; **The Steward's Testimony** — her honest account of the hollow years, delivered once, should cost the party's certainty more than any battle.)*

## GM Notes

**She is the argument the party must beat.** Krane is the king's case made flesh in Forgeholm; Iveth is the harder version, because her evidence is *true*: the old Agoran really was hollow ([the Heart drank it](../../world/anchors-and-engines.md#agoran--the-warm-heart-now-the-kings-heart)), and the king's warmth really works. The party cannot win Commonhold by telling her she's deceived about what she felt. They win by explaining it better than the king does — the extraction, the sixty stolen years, the flame that rose because it was *re-aimed*, not because he refilled it. The moment Iveth understands that her childhood emptiness was a theft and the king's warmth is the same theft pointed at a crown, the Hall of Names changes sides — and the Hall of Names is Commonhold.

**The two graces.** The climax's central scene runs through her ([Commonhold — GM Notes](../../locations/commonhold.md#gm-notes)): Oren at the Ring with the old grace, the king's liturgy against it, and Iveth — the trained voice of the city, the one who knows both by heart — deciding which one Commonhold sings. Set it up all campaign: the party should know about the unstruck name before the end, and Oren should know who kept it. Teacher and prentice, the walkout and the one who stayed, singing at last from the same page is the liberation of Agoran in a single image.

**If pushed too fast:** she reports them. Not from cruelty — from loyalty and terror in equal parts; the party's questions sound exactly like the hollow feeling she has spent five years grateful to be rid of. An early, clumsy approach turns the warmest door in Commonhold into the quiet corps' best source on the party. Earn her slowly: eat at her table, mean it, and let her *watch the party braid* — the one warmth in the world with no king in the circuit.
