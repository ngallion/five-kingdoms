# Warden-General Bram Harrow

**Role:** Commander of the King's Wardens — Tier 3 lieutenant; the shepherd who has never counted his wolves
**Location:** The Musterfields, [Commonhold](../../locations/commonhold.md), between circuits; in the field more than any general alive
**First appeared:** *(not yet — best introduced saving the party's lives)*
**Status:** Alive, beloved in five kingdoms, and one honest map away from ruin

## Appearance

Fifties, plains-weathered, built like a gatepost. Grey cloak gone nearly white with wear — he refuses a new one; the corps reads the old cloak the way Forgeholm reads marks. Moves and speaks with the unhurried economy of a man who has done every job he orders done. Carries a warden's long-axe and a horn older than the kingdom's fall, and knows the name of every rider in every company, which is several thousand names.

## Personality

The real thing. Harrow is brave, plain, generous, and entirely without appetite for glory — the finest fighter of march-things alive, and the first man through every breach anyway. He believes *no one stands alone* the way Keeper Damet believes in Tavan: as the one true sentence in the world. He mourns every village lost in any kingdom as if it were Agoran's, rides to pleas the king never hears about, and treats the rescued of four kingdoms with a courtesy that has done more for Korrven's cause than any garrison. The corps would follow him into the Hollow March. Several times, it has.

## Backstory

A border warden's son from the Thornfell edge. In the slow years before the surge, he held a village called **Harrow's Fold** — his own people — through a bad winter against the things drifting off the moors, and lost it anyway: he ordered the beacon lit, help never came, and he walked out of the thaw the only survivor. The inquiry blamed no one, which everyone understood to mean him. He was three seasons into drinking himself off the rolls when Korrven — newly welcomed, gathering Agoran's broken things — came and sat with him, listened to the whole story without once flinching, and said: *the help will come, because you will build it.* The King's Wardens are Harrow's answer to one unanswered beacon. Every rescue is that night, ridden again, arriving in time.

He has commanded five years of the surge without losing a single Agoran village, and nearly every plea abroad that his riders could reach. He is aware his roads are miraculous. He calls it the king's providence and the corps' discipline, out loud, and rides harder, and does not finish the thought.

## Relationship to the Party

They should meet him as a rescuer — grey cloaks out of the fog in some kingdom's wilds, the wall held, grain left behind, no fee and no questions ([the Wardens](../../world/the-hollowing.md#the-kings-wardens)). He will like the party if they fight well and stand their ground for strangers; his respect is easy to earn and appalling to hold, given what it serves. Later, when the party stands between the king and a kingdom, Harrow is what the campaign sends against them — the best man they know, on the wrong side, arriving in time.

## Secrets

- **He signs the Survey.** The Dousers' route-orders move under his seal, batched among honest beacon-schedules and "consolidations" ([the mechanism](../../world/the-hollowing.md#gm-notes)). He believes — has arranged to believe — that the Survey is triage: the tide must go somewhere, and routing it is mercy's arithmetic. He has never laid the Survey's routes against the attack reports. Both maps hang in his own Musterfields. Different rooms.
- **Harrow's Fold is in the ledger.** The oldest entries in the Survey's files predate the corps — the quiet work began before the Wardens existed, in the last years before the Fall, when the king's gathering camps needed the roads *around* them dark. One doused beacon that winter stands between a Thornfell camp and a village called Harrow's Fold. Whether Korrven knew what that particular dousing cost — whether he sat down beside the man his own quiet work had broken and recruited the grief he manufactured — is the cruelest question in the campaign. Decide late. *(If yes, Vael has known all along, and has been saving it.)*
- He keeps the horn from the Fold on his belt and has never sounded it. The corps thinks it's sentiment. It's evidence — he is waiting to sound it the night he finally arrives in time for his own village, which is never.

## Daggerheart Stats

**Tier:** 3 (Leader — the finest conventional commander in the five kingdoms)
**Difficulty:** 17
**Thresholds:** 18 / 35 · **HP:** 9 · **Stress:** 6
**Attacks/Moves:**
- *Warden's long-axe* — melee, ~3d10+4 phy
- **No One Stands Alone** (passive): while any ally is within Close range of Harrow, attacks against that ally are at disadvantage. His companies fight in his shape.
- **The Horn Unwound** (action, once per fight): every warden within Far range clears a Stress and moves; the line reforms. Describe it — men who were breaking, not breaking.
- **Arrive in Time** (reaction, spend a Fear): Harrow appears where a PC or NPC is about to fall — between the blow and the body. He takes the hit.
- **The General's Word** (spend 2 Fear): a rescued town, a grateful garrison, or a warden company the party counted as neutral takes the field on his order. He is owed by half the world.

*(Fighting Harrow head-on should feel like fighting the best of the setting's virtues. Prefer staging where the party must break his faith, not his line.)*

## GM Notes

**He is the reveal's hinge.** The herding proof ([The Hollowing — GM Notes](../../world/the-hollowing.md#gm-notes)) indicts the king with every kingdom at once — but its *delivery mechanism* is Harrow: the corps follows him, not Korrven, and four kingdoms' worth of rescued villages trust his cloak. The proof shown to the world without Harrow makes a war. The proof shown to *Harrow* first can make a defection that takes the Wardens — the king's whole public face — out of Korrven's hands in a single night.

**The scene is the map.** Don't hand him documents — make him draw it. The party that has the Survey ledger and the attack reports should get him to a table and have him lay one against the other with his own hands ([the two rooms](#secrets)). Play it slow. The moment lands when he reaches Harrow's Fold's beacon in the old entries and understands, at last, why help never came — the king found him *because* the king broke him. What happens in the next breath is a table-defining scene: grief, the horn, and one of three roads.

**Three roads:** he **turns** — sounds the horn, and the corps splits or comes over (best case; earn it); he **shatters** — doubles down because the ledger's truth would unmake every rescue since the Fold, and becomes the Tier 3 boss of the Agoran approach, fighting the party with a dead man's eyes; or he **goes to the king** — walks into the Ring with the ledger and asks Korrven to his face, which Korrven cannot afford and Vael will not permit; what comes back out of that audience wearing Harrow's cloak is a problem the party made. Foreshadow all three; let play choose.

**Keep his heroism unironic.** Every rescue is real, every grateful village genuinely owes him, and most of what the King's Wardens do is exactly what it appears to be. The horror of Harrow is not hypocrisy — it is that the best man in the setting built his life's redemption on the machinery that required his ruin. If the players stop admiring him, the tragedy stops working; keep him admirable to the last scene.
