# Edda Prine, the Last Reader

**Role:** Underkeeper of the Deep Stacks — the only living person who can read the oldest texts in the world; gatekeeper of the Veritas arc
**Location:** The bottom of the Great Archive, [Meridian](../../locations/meridian.md) — the lamp beside the First Shelves
**First appeared:** *(not yet — planned for the Veritas arc)*
**Status:** Dying — slowly, without complaint, with one telling left in her

## Appearance

Eighties, small, wrapped in the layered wool of someone who has worked fifty years in the coldest rooms in the world. Moves through the dark stacks without a lamp lit — she carries it unlit and lights it only to read. Her hands are the tell: ruined knuckles, immaculate care — she handles a two-thousand-year-old page the way other people handle a sleeping child. Up close she smells of cold paper and the peppermint she chews for a heart that skips.

## Personality

Precise, dry, and almost silent — but her silence is not the kingdom's silence. Veritas hoards; Prine *guards*. She stopped speaking her work decades ago the way a soldier stops naming what's in the powder-room: not from greed but from a very exact understanding of what she is standing next to. She has a sense of humor no one has seen in years (Annot Fell gets one dry sentence a week, and treasures them), no patience whatsoever for rank, and one unspent tenderness: anyone who asks a *real question* — not "what do you know" but "how does it work" — gets three more minutes of her than anybody has gotten in a decade.

## Backstory

A Stacks-crew child who never left. Prine was shelving the deep galleries at twelve, Underkeeper by forty, and somewhere in between she did the thing no ranked scholar would waste standing on: she taught herself the **Old Tongue** — the mundanely dead language of the deepest shelves — alone, over forty years, from cognates, bilingual fragments, and stubbornness. (Possible *because* it is mundanely dead: the Concord Tongue's magic-kept grammar slides off every mind alike, but the Old Tongue is merely extinct, and yields to one lifetime of patience.)

What she found in it she has never told a soul: the **Vael references** — text after text across dead centuries, different authors, different lands, always the same shape: *a vast, patient emptiness drawn to isolation the way a wound is drawn to infection* ([Vael, the Unmade](../../world/vael-the-unmade.md)). And then, these last five years, she watched the surveys she isn't supposed to see and the fringe-town reports nobody collates, and understood that the shape in the oldest pages and the shape emptying the [Screes](../../world/geography.md#the-screes-forgeholm--veritas) are the same shape, moving.

Why silent? Three reasons, layered like her wool. She has felt the flinch — small tellings, early on, each one leaving her lighter in the worst way — and worked out the rule the whole kingdom lives by without stating: **she can give the understanding of her lifetime once, whole, and lose it in the giving.** She is terrified of the wrong ear getting the only reading of Vael ever made. And — the true one, underneath — no one has ever asked. Fifty years, the answer to the world's oldest question, and not one person has come down the stairs wanting anything but a shelf-mark.

## Relationship to the Party

The gatekeeper who wants to be opened. Prine controls access to the First Shelves in every practical sense (Corle holds the key; Prine knows which key, which gallery, which crate — and which cold "runs different"). She will test the party the only way she knows: with questions. Answer *how does it work* honestly — including "we don't know yet" — and she begins, one dry sentence at a time, to consider them. She is also the arc's clock made human: her heart skips, winter is coming to the coldest rooms in the world, and [Ansel Marr](ansel-marr.md)'s letters — kind, exact, genuinely understanding — are already in her drawer.

## Secrets

- **The tally of forgetting.** She has read the six-tariff ledgers ([History — What Historians Got Wrong](../../world/history.md#what-historians-got-wrong)) and watched the sixth name slide out of her own memory. So she ran the experiment: a tally-sheet, one mark per reading, kept with the ledger. There are over four hundred marks. She remembers making none of them. It is the most frightening object in the Archive, and the fastest way to show a player character the unmaking is real: hand them the tally and let them make the four-hundred-and-first mark.
- **She knows what the Preservation Engine is not.** Fifty years beside "the draft" — she has no theory of Hearts, but she knows the cold near the First Shelves *pays attention*, and she has arranged the shelving, quietly, over decades, so the oldest texts sit just outside its reach.
- **Marr's offer is the exact shape of her wound.** Not rank, not money: *an amanuensis corps in Commonhold, her reading taken down entire, her name on the Concordance's first volume — understood at last, before the end.* Every word of it delivers, too. That is the horror. If she goes north, or he comes down her stairs enough times, the only reading of Vael ever made goes to the thing it describes — spent into one kind ear, and gone.

## GM Notes

**The one-telling mechanic.** Play her limit as hard canon: one whole telling, then the understanding is gone from her — the Silent Heart drinks the transfer. This makes *where and how she tells it* the arc's central tactical question, with a rising ladder the party can discover: told in her stacks, the telling is spent and she is emptied (the knowledge survives only in the hearers — who must then hold it communally, like everything else the unmaking hunts). Told at a [Loosening](../../world/factions/the-wayfair.md#the-loosening) — the fair camped beyond the Hem — the grip slackens and she keeps perhaps half. Told at [the Reading](../../locations/meridian.md#gm-notes) itself, braided into the first shared discovery in two centuries with fellowship holding the room, **she keeps it all**: the first teacher in Veritas in living memory to finish a lecture larger than she began it. The best version of the arc saves the knowledge *and* the woman, and the party should be able to figure out that those were never two different problems.

**She is the proof-of-method NPC.** Luminar has Vess Tallow — the native practice that already works. Veritas has Prine inverted: the native practice that *almost* worked alone, and cannot finish alone. Her forty years cracked the Old Tongue solo; the Concord Tongue will not yield to her or anyone solo, ever, and she knows it — she has copied its glyphs and lost the grammar by morning like everyone else. When she finally says out loud that the last door in her Archive can only be opened by *people who trust each other*, the kingdom's thesis is spoken by its greatest hoarder.

**If the party never comes for her:** Marr does. Winter, the skipping heart, the kind letters — she gives the telling to the Correspondent's amanuenses by spring, dies lighter and smiling, and the campaign's deepest intelligence asset arrives in Commonhold crated beside the First Shelves. Do not soften this on the clock's account; soften it only if the party has *earned* the warning. Annot Fell knows the old woman is failing. Whether anyone ever asks Annot is the kind of thing campaigns turn on.
